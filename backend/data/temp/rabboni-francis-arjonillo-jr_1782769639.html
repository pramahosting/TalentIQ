    My Work Toolstack changed completely.

    In just 12 months.

    A year ago, I used generic tools just like everyone else

    Now? I have replaced every single one with something better

    Why? Why waste time when AI is here to expedite your work for you

    Here's what changed from January 2025 to January 2026:

    Research:
    Wikipedia → ChatGPT

    Writing:
    ChatGPT → Claude

    Infographics:
    Canva → Gemini Nano Banana Pro

    Presentations:
    Microsoft PowerPoint → Gamma

    Meetings:
    Read AI → Granola

    Keyboard Typing:
    Physical keyboard → Wispr Flow

    Designs:
    Figma → Magic Patterns

    Prototyping:
    Pen and paper → Lovable

    Productivity System:
    Microsoft Office → PD's Personal OS (Cursor + Claude Code + Github)

    That last one is the biggest shift. I don't even even a file directly anymore.

    I went from using a collection of disconnected tools to building a complete operating system for how I work.

    Why this matters:
    ↳ The AI landscape moves fast. What worked 12 months ago is already outdated.
    ↳ Most people still use the same tools they started with, probably at the start of their careers.
    ↳ They're not bad tools. They're just not the most useful anymore.

    The pattern I've noticed:
    ↳ Every ~3 months, a new tool emerges that's 10x better
    ↳ Early adopters win because they compound advantages
    ↳ The gap between "I'll learn it later" and "I'm using it now" gets wider

    Here's my rule: Every quarter, I review my entire stack.

    I ask myself: Is there something better?

    If yes, I test it for a week.

    If it saves time or improves output in multiples, I switch immediately.

    That's how I went from generic to specialized in such a short time.

    Ever tried understanding what a Business Analyst does in the IT world?

    “No coding? But still in tech?” — a common reaction. 😅

    Here’s what a BA actually does:
    ✅ Translates vague client ideas into structured, actionable requirements
    ✅ Acts as the bridge between business goals and technical execution
    ✅ Asks “Why?” until the real problem is uncovered
    ✅ Guards the project from scope creep like a silent superhero
    ✅ Ensures the final product solves the right problem, not just what was initially requested

    Why Product Owners Need Business Analysts

    In today’s fast-paced digital landscape, product owners are under constant pressure to deliver value swiftly and strategically. While they drive product vision and stakeholder alignment, business analysts (BAs) are crucial partners who help translate that vision into actionable requirements. Without BAs, product owners often get buried in the weeds—juggling user stories, edge cases, and competing priorities.

    Business analysts serve as the bridge between business needs and technical teams. They dive deep into data, workflows, and stakeholder feedback to uncover hidden requirements, edge cases, and opportunities. Their structured analysis ensures that the product backlog isn’t just filled—it’s focused and prioritized for maximum impact.

    Moreover, BAs excel at facilitating clear communication across departments. They reduce misunderstandings between stakeholders and developers by creating detailed documentation, user stories, and acceptance criteria that reflect real-world use. This clarity minimizes costly rework and ensures that delivered features solve the right problems.

    Ultimately, while product owners steer the ship, business analysts help chart the course and keep it on track. Together, they form a dynamic duo that transforms vision into value—faster, smarter, and more effectively.

    I often see job titles like:
    Business Analyst – Payments
    Business Analyst – BFSI
    Business Analyst – Insurance

    And every time, I wonder…
    Why don’t we just say, “We need a solid Business Analyst”?

    Because honestly, being a good BA isn’t about the domain—it’s about the skills.

    A real BA knows how to:

    * Understand what the business really needs
    * Gather and translate requirements
    * Keep everyone on the same page
    * Communicate clearly
    * Solve problems logically
    * Create docs that make sense to both business and tech teams
    * Make sure the solution aligns with the bigger goals

    Sure, domain knowledge helps—it’s a bonus.
    But is it a must? Nope.

    A good BA can pick up any domain. Every client and project is different anyway. The core BA skills work everywhere.

    So maybe we should stop asking, “Does he knows this industry?”
    and start asking, “Can he think, analyze, and deliver?”

    What do you think? Have you switched domains as a BA and made it work?
